Background and Motivation

On the first day of class, when we were asked which social issue we cared most about, we both mentioned food insecurity and the importance of ensuring that everyone has access to basic nutritional needs. That shared interest became the driving force behind our project idea.

Despite record levels of global food production, millions of people continue to face chronic hunger. Our goal is to visualize this imbalance and make the scale of the issue tangible. This topic is especially relevant in the current global context, where conflict and economic instability are exacerbating hunger in certain regions. In some cases, food is even used as a weapon, with populations being intentionally deprived of resources. By visualizing where and why food insecurity persists, we hope to spark greater awareness and encourage data-driven discussions around solutions such as reducing waste, improving distribution, and increasing sustainable production.

Project Objectives

Primary questions:

  • What is the current severity of food insecurity in different regions of the world?
  • How does the current volume of food production and the current volume of food loss/waste compare across regions?
  • Which regions appear to be handling food production and distribution more efficiently (i.e., high production + low waste + lower hunger)? Which regions appear most vulnerable (higher waste or low production + high insecurity)?
  • What might the inefficiencies suggest about possible interventions (i.e. is there a better way to distribute food)?

What we would like to learn and accomplish:

  • Determine whether the world currently produces enough food to meet global nutritional needs.
  • Identify regions where food insecurity remains high despite high food production, revealing possible inequities or inefficiencies in distribution.
  • Compare food production, waste, and insecurity on a single visual platform to show how these factors interact.

Benefits:

  • Shows if global hunger is more impacted by production or an inefficient allocation of resources.
  • Raises awareness to a complex issue and begins a discussion to come up with solutions.

Key Metrics:

Visualization Design

Sketch 1

pA Sankey diagram illustrating global food flow. We also have a dropdown menu so users can compare changes over time. An additional bar graph compares global production to recommended dietary proportions to visualize over/underproduction patterns.

  • Pros:
    • Effectively shows food flow from production to consumption/waste
    • Clear quantitative comparisons since nodes/links will be labeled with exact values
  • Cons:
    • Does not provide clear insights into food insecurity
    • Less geographic and spatial information compared to the world map approach
Sketch 2

A world map where production is shown as a product type appearing in the country it is being produced in. Food insecurity is shown as as the different shades of red. Food waste is shown as an accumulating bar on the side

  • Pros:
    • Easy to compare how food waste and food insecurity relate
    • Geographic context for production and insecurity
  • Cons:
    • Can be too much cluster

Sketch 3

A world map where users can switch between layers showing food production, food insecurity, and food waste. We will use a tooltip on hover to show more detailed values for specific regions

  • Pros:
    • Straightforward and easy to read
    • Clear geographic context for regional comparisons
  • Cons:
    • Harder to compare/contrast the connection between the 3 views
    • Quantitative precision is limited at first glance

Final Design

The final visualization uses a Sankey diagram and supporting visuals. We chose a Sankey diagram to illustrate the flow of food from each continent into the outcomes: food consumed and food wasted. We chose this over the world map visualizations because they were less successful at conveying food flow and immediate quantitative data. The bar charts visually compare the total tons of food wasted to the estimated tons required to solve food insecurity. We also added an additional visualization that breaks down waste percentages by food category for additional nuance and connection to systemic inefficiencies.

Must-Have Features

  • Easy-to-read metrics for total food insecurity, food waste, and food production
  • Display showing how many pounds of food are required to resolve food insecurity
  • Interactive hover tooltip to view metrics by continent

Optional Features

  • Animations or smooth transitions between views
  • Breakdown of food waste by type (to distinguish perishable vs. non-perishable items for distribution feasibility)
  • Bar chart showing suggested consumption of each food type
  • Data filtering options (e.g., filter by production of a specific food type)
  • Food insecurity bar visualization that can toggle between global totals and regional/continent-level breakdowns
